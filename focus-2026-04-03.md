## 3. Salesforce Source-of-Truth Cutover — Signal Suppression Is the Landmine

**Sources:**
- [Notion PR/FAQ: Salesforce Sales Source of Truth](https://www.notion.so/321b9be2b0f381348cf3fd5385097f2f)
- [Slack #emerging-products](https://yourcounterpart.slack.com/archives/C089Q68S484/p1772566746527019)

Django Admin goes read-only for broker data; Salesforce becomes the write origin via the new `BrokerInboundSyncService` at `/salesforce-api/brokers/sync/`. Four feature flags gate the cutover sequence. The critical pre-go-live risks: SyncID backfill, split-brain resolution, and — most importantly — `ignore_signals` must be airtight. A gap in signal suppression will spam users with welcome emails on every sync. This architectural inversion is the highest-stakes coordination item in the current sprint.

## 5. LiteLLM Supply Chain Attack — Audit Status Unknown

**Sources:**
- [Slack #ai-security](https://yourcounterpart.slack.com/archives/C_ai-security) (March 25)
- [LiteLLM Supply Chain Attack writeup](https://www.comet.com/site/blog/litellm-supply-chain-attack/)

Chad Nierenhausen flagged a Python supply chain attack targeting LiteLLM on March 25. The required actions were: (1) audit all codebases for direct and transitive LiteLLM dependencies, and (2) rotate any API keys that could have been exfiltrated. This item has not resurfaced in subsequent digests — which likely means it was handled, but is worth a quick confirmation that the audit completed and any necessary key rotations were done. Given how many LLM provider keys a platform like Counterpart holds, an unresolved exposure here would be high severity.
